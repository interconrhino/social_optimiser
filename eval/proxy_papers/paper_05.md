# Who Should Our Advisors Call This Month? — Keeping Smallholder Farmers Engaged with a Climate-Smart Agriculture Advisory Service

*Brief prepared by the Director of Farmer Services, GreenFurrow Cooperative Trust — for internal review and external technical partners, April 2026*

## Background on our organisation

GreenFurrow is a farmer-facing non-profit operating across three districts of a semi-arid region. For the past six years we have run a free **monthly voice-advisory service** for smallholder farmers: each enrolled farmer receives a short automated voice message on the first of every month, in their local language, with practical guidance tailored to the season — when to sow, how to rotate crops under this year's rainfall forecast, which pest pressure to expect, how to prepare soil between cycles, and which government subsidy windows are open. The content is produced by a small team of agronomists and pushed out through a telecom partnership that reaches even basic-phone users.

The advisory is the core of what we do. It's free to the farmer, it's in a language they actually speak, and — when farmers actually listen to it and act on it — the outcomes are real: households on the advisory for a full year have measurably better yields, less pesticide waste, and higher income stability than comparable households outside the programme. We have that data from a multi-year observational study and a small RCT done with a university partner.

## The problem we want help with

We currently have just over **28,000 farmers enrolled** across the three districts. Enrolment is growing. The structural problem is one we have known about for years but have never solved well: **a large fraction of farmers stop listening.** The monthly voice message goes out; their phone rings; they may pick up for ten seconds and hang up, or not pick up at all, or listen to the first line and set the phone down. Once a farmer stops engaging with the monthly message for two or three months in a row, they almost never come back on their own, and we lose all the downstream benefit for that household.

To fight this, we employ **12 farmer-support advisors** — real people, based in the district offices, who speak the local languages. Their job, among other things, is to place **personal follow-up calls** to enrolled farmers: a friendly ten-minute call that checks in on the farmer, answers questions, asks whether the last month's advisory was useful, and nudges them back into the habit of listening. These calls demonstrably bring farmers back: a farmer who was drifting away from the programme and then gets one of these personal calls is noticeably more likely to be listening to the automated message again the following month.

The hard part is that we cannot call everybody. Realistically, across the 12 advisors, we can place around **400 personal follow-up calls per month** — that is the sustainable sustainable throughput given call duration, travel between district offices, and the other duties these advisors have. **400 calls against 28,000 farmers.** The question we need to answer every month is: **which 400 farmers should the advisors call this month?**

## What we do today, and why it isn't good enough

At the moment the 400 monthly calls are allocated by a rule that our field director set up five years ago and that has never really been revisited. It is a **systematic rotation**: we keep a list of all enrolled farmers ordered by enrolment date, and every month we take the next 400 names off the top of the queue (wrapping around when we reach the end). In parallel, any farmer who calls our helpline with a complaint or a specific question gets a follow-up call added on top of that quota.

The rotation rule is easy to explain to advisors and auditors, and it is unambiguously fair in a procedural sense: over a long horizon, every farmer gets called roughly the same number of times. But it is almost certainly leaving benefit on the table. It calls people who are listening fine and don't need a nudge. It ignores early warning signs — a farmer who listened for eight months straight and then suddenly stopped is treated the same as a farmer who has been steadily engaged. And because we've never formally compared it to anything else, we cannot tell our funders how much good those 400 calls per month are actually doing relative to simply doing nothing.

We want to replace the rotation with a smarter monthly selection, **and we want the replacement to be validated in the field against what we do today**, not just in a simulation. Our board is willing to fund a pilot where part of the farmer base is handled by the new approach and another part stays on the current rotation, so that at the end of the pilot we can make an honest side-by-side comparison.

## What success looks like

At the end of a 6-to-8 month pilot, we want to be able to say, with statistical backing, something like: *"Under the new monthly selection approach, the total number of farmers who remained actively engaged with the monthly advisory over the pilot window was meaningfully higher than under the rotation, at the same 400-calls-per-month budget."* We care about cumulative engagement over the whole window, not any single month — a farmer who is kept engaged for all eight months matters a lot more than one brought back for a single month and then lost again.

We also care about failure modes of the rule we're replacing: are there entire sub-groups of farmers the rotation is systematically under-serving? Any replacement must not quietly cut off certain villages or language groups.

## Data we can make available

- **Five years of monthly engagement logs** for every enrolled farmer — for each month, whether the farmer listened to the automated message, for how many seconds, and whether an advisor follow-up call was placed that month. This is about 28,000 farmers × 60 months, though many farmers enrolled partway through.
- **Advisor call logs** — when the call was placed, which advisor, duration, and a short structured note on whether the call reached the farmer and how the conversation went.
- **Enrolment information per farmer** — district, village, preferred language, age band, farm size category, crop mix, education level, whether they have a smartphone or a basic phone, distance to nearest district office, and whether they are part of a local farmer collective.
- **Seasonal context** — rainfall and market-price data at the district level, month by month.

We do not have a clean historical experiment where calls were assigned randomly — historically, who got a call in any given month was decided by the rotation rule plus ad-hoc helpline-driven calls, so our data reflects that policy, not a random one. We know this is a constraint on what can be learned from the historical logs alone, and we'd like the technical approach to be honest about that.

## What we're asking for

A defensible monthly selection procedure that our advisors can use in the field, together with a pilot design that lets us validate it against the current rotation on real farmers over a real season. Our technical capacity in-house is modest: two data analysts comfortable with Python and SQL, no in-house optimisation expertise. We can commit analyst time and a pilot budget; we cannot commit to a black-box system that the field director cannot explain to an advisor or a district auditor.
