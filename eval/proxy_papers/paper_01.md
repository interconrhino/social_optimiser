# Help us make our student-success outreach fair to everyone we serve

**From:** Maria Alcantara, Director of Student Success, Rio Valle Community College Network
**To:** prospective data partners

## Who we are

Rio Valle is a network of four community colleges across a mid-sized state. Between our four campuses we enrol around 18,000 students each semester, most of whom are juggling coursework with jobs, caregiving, or a long commute. Our Student Success office runs a weekly outreach programme: trained success coaches call students who look like they might be drifting, to check in, troubleshoot a specific obstacle (financial-aid form stuck, childcare falling through, a class they are afraid to drop), and nudge them back into engagement with their classes.

The programme works. Students who get a well-timed call stay enrolled at meaningfully higher rates than students who do not. The problem we are writing to you about is not whether to do the calls — it is **which students to call each week**, because we can only call a tiny fraction of them.

## The decision we have to make every week

Each of our four campuses has a pool of roughly 500–700 students flagged as "at some risk" in any given week (so about 2,100 across the network at any time). Our total coaching capacity across the network is about 210 outreach calls per week — roughly one-tenth of the flagged pool. Every Monday morning, our coaches sit down and have to decide: of the ~2,100 students on the watch-list, which 210 do we actually call this week?

We do this every week through the 12-week semester.

The students on the watch-list are not static. A student who is fine this week might be in a rough patch three weeks from now; a student we call this week might stabilise and not need another call for a month; a student we leave alone might slip from "at some risk" to "actively disengaged" before we get around to them. We cannot directly see how engaged each student is — we infer it from late assignments, login patterns, last time their advisor heard from them — but the true picture is fuzzy. Our weekly call decisions have to work with that fuzziness.

## What makes this hard: our stakeholders disagree about who to prioritise

We have three funders and an internal equity committee, and they do not all want the same thing. Every semester, our leadership sits down and tries to write a one-paragraph prioritisation statement for the coaching team. A real example from last semester read roughly:

> *"This semester please give extra priority to first-generation students and to students from low-income zip codes, while also not reducing outreach to our adult learners (age 25+) relative to last semester."*

That sounds reasonable when you read it, but when the coaches actually try to act on it, the three clauses pull in different directions. First-generation students skew younger; low-income zip codes correlate with certain campuses; protecting outreach to adult learners means fewer calls available for the first two groups. Last semester one coach built a simple scoring rule off that paragraph; at the end of the semester we discovered that outreach to students without dependants had quietly dropped by 30% as a side-effect, which nobody intended and which the provost was unhappy about.

We need help translating these plain-English priority statements — which change every semester and which different stakeholders write differently — into an actual, defensible weekly call list. And we need to be able to **show our board** how a given priority statement was turned into a call list, because "the algorithm said so" is not going to fly with our equity committee.

## What data we have

- Three years of weekly engagement records for ~12,000 former students: weekly "engagement level" (a 0/1 proxy derived from LMS activity and assignment submission), and a log of every coaching call placed.
- Demographic and intake features for every student: age band, income band (from FAFSA), first-gen status, home campus, programme of study, dependants, prior-term GPA band, distance-from-campus band. Typically 8–12 features per student.
- Current-semester weekly data on the active ~18,000 students.

The historical call logs reflect the judgement of past coaches, not a random assignment — so the data is informative but biased.

## What success looks like for us

A system where:
1. Our leadership writes a plain-English prioritisation paragraph at the start of each semester.
2. The system turns that paragraph into a weekly call-list recipe that our coaches can actually use.
3. We can show, for any given recipe, *how well it honours each clause of the paragraph* — and we can choose whether we want to balance the clauses evenly, protect the worst-served group, or maximise overall impact.
4. We can see and flag unintended side-effects (e.g. "this recipe quietly deprioritises commuter students") *before* we deploy it, not after.

If you think you can help us build something like this, we would love to talk.
